Visualizing Exchange Rates¶

The relative value of currencies are quantified using exchange rates. For instance, a Euro-USD exchange rate of 2.0 means that 1 euro will get you 2 US dollar. In this notebook, we'll be investigating a dataset that contains euro exchange rates for many different countries. In particular, we'll compare the exchange rate from the Euro to the US Dollar (USD) with the exchange rate from the Euro to the British Pound Sterling (GBP).

Summary¶

After cleaning the data, we created a visualization comparing the Euro-USD exchange rate with the Euro-GBP. This visualization serves as an explanation of the well known fact that the British Pound Sterling is pegged to US Dollar. This means that fluctuations in the Euro-USD exchange rate will cause proportional fluctations in the Euro-GBP exchange rate, at a slight time lag.

About the Data¶

The dataset used in this notebook can be found on kaggle. It contains daily euro exhcange rates for over 40 countries from 1999 to 2021.

Here are some initial observations about the dataset:

  • The column names are in the form: [Country currency ]. This format should be fixed to something more intuitive.
  • The Period/Unit column corresponds to time, and is currently a string.
  • Many columns have a large number of missing values.

To begin the cleaning process, let's rename the US dollar and UK pounds sterling columns to something more intuitive. We'll also rename the Period//Unit column to Time, and change it to a datetime datatype.

In [ ]:
exchange_rates.rename(columns={'[US dollar ]': 'US_dollar',
                                '[UK pound sterling ]': 'UK_pound',
                               'Period\\Unit:': 'Time'},
                      inplace=True)
exchange_rates['Time'] = pd.to_datetime(exchange_rates['Time'])

Now, we'll sort the values in the dataframe according to the time column, and reset the index. We'll also restrict to a dataframe with three columns, since in this notebook we'll be looking how the US Dollar and British Pound Sterling compare to the Euro.

In [ ]:
exchange_rates.sort_values('Time', inplace=True) 
exchange_rates.reset_index(drop=True, inplace=True)
exchange_rates = exchange_rates.copy()[['Time', 'US_dollar','UK_pound', 'Russian_rouble']]

From the above, its clear that the dashes do occur in the same rows. In the following code cell, we simply filter out all rows containing the '-' character, and then convert the country columns to float.

In [ ]:
exchange_rates = exchange_rates.copy()[~exchange_rates['US_dollar'].str.contains('-')]
for col in ['US_dollar','UK_pound']:
    exchange_rates[col] = exchange_rates[col].astype(float)

Data Visualization¶

Now that the errant values have been removed, we're ready to create a visualization.

In [ ]:
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
plt.style.use('fivethirtyeight')
colors = ['mediumseagreen','orangered']
fig,ax = plt.subplots(figsize = (12,8))
for i,col in enumerate(['US_dollar','UK_pound']):  #'Russian_rouble']:
    ax.plot(exchange_rates['Time'], exchange_rates[col].rolling(30).mean(), label = col, alpha = .5, color = colors[i])

ax.tick_params(colors = 'grey', which = 'both')

While the largest peak in the Euro-USD exchange rate corresponds to the 2008 finnancial crisis, we also see that the Euro-GPB exchange rate jumped during that time period. Moreover, looking closely we see that peaks and troughs are actually in a one-to-one correspondence bewteen the two currencies. This is evidence of the well known fact that the GBP is pegged to the US Dollar.
